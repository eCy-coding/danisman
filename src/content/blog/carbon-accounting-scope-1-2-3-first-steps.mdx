For Turkish companies, carbon accounting is no longer merely an ESG-reporting exercise. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) entered its definitive regime on 1 January 2026 — imports of covered goods from that date carry a financial liability for certain sectors, even though the purchase and surrender of CBAM certificates only begins in 2027 — supply-chain requests from EU customers are intensifying, and financial institutions are increasingly weighing carbon data in their lending decisions. This article sets out where to begin across the Scope 1, 2, and 3 categories.

## Scope concepts in brief

The GHG Protocol — the internationally accepted greenhouse-gas accounting standard — divides emissions into three categories. **Scope 1** covers direct emissions from sources the company owns or controls (boilers, generators, vehicle fleets, fugitive leaks). **Scope 2** covers indirect emissions from purchased energy — electricity, heating, cooling. **Scope 3** covers value-chain emissions: all other indirect emissions, both upstream (suppliers) and downstream (customers, product use, waste), further divided into fifteen subcategories.

In many companies, Scope 3 is estimated to account for 70–90 percent of the total carbon footprint. It is nonetheless the hardest category to measure with precision.

## Scope 1: fuel and direct emissions

Data collection here is comparatively straightforward. The starting point is the company's monthly bills for directly consumed fuel:

- **Natural gas:** m³ consumed × a conversion factor, drawn from a recognized emission-factor reference such as the IPCC default factors used in Türkiye's national greenhouse-gas inventory.
- **Diesel:** liters × approximately 2.68 kg CO2e/L.
- **Gasoline:** liters × approximately 2.31 kg CO2e/L.
- **LPG:** kilograms × approximately 2.99 kg CO2e/kg.
- **Refrigerant leaks:** gases such as R-134a and R-410a each carry a different Global Warming Potential (GWP) multiplier; leak volume is typically estimated from annual maintenance-service records.

Typical data sources are the accounting department (fuel purchase invoices), the fleet-management system, generator logs, and refrigeration maintenance reports. Most companies begin in a spreadsheet and later integrate the process into their ERP system.

## Scope 2: purchased energy

Two methods are used for Scope 2 accounting in Türkiye:

1. **Location-based:** kWh consumed × Türkiye's average grid emission factor (published annually by the Ministry of Energy and Natural Resources — on the order of 0.43–0.47 kg CO2e/kWh).
2. **Market-based:** zero emissions for the kWh volume covered by owned renewable-energy certificates (YEK-G, I-REC), with the grid factor applied to the remainder.

The CSRD (the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive) and comparable frameworks expect both figures to be reported side by side. Purchasing renewable-energy certificates can bring Scope 2 to zero, but it has no bearing on Scope 3 — and if the certificate volume is overstated relative to actual consumption, it invites a greenwashing challenge.

Data sources: monthly electricity bills (the kWh line) and YEK-G/I-REC purchase documentation.

## Scope 3: fifteen categories, three practical approaches

Scope 3 is the most daunting category, since extending data coverage across all fifteen subcategories requires an infrastructure that typically takes years to build. Aiming for "full accuracy" in the first year is not a realistic target. Three practical approaches are available:

**Approach 1: spend-based methodology.** Annual supplier spend (in TRY or USD) multiplied by a sector-level emission-intensity factor — for instance, on the order of 0.5 kg CO2e per USD spent in an "industrial machinery" category — drawing on Environmentally Extended Input-Output (EEIO) tables. Fast to apply, but imprecise.

**Approach 2: average-data methodology.** An average emission factor per unit of product or service purchased — for example, one ton of purchased steel taken as roughly 1.8 tons of CO2e. More accurate, but it requires a factor for every product category.

**Approach 3: supplier-specific data.** A unit emission factor drawn from the supplier's own carbon disclosure. The most accurate option, but it depends on supplier participation; large Turkish suppliers do not yet report this data as a rule, and this approach is more likely to mature over the next three to five years.

In practice, a staged sequence works well: Approach 1 in year one, Approach 2 applied to the top twenty suppliers in year two, and a gradual shift toward Approach 3 from year three onward.

## Data-collection infrastructure

Three infrastructure tiers are worth distinguishing:

- **Spreadsheet plus manual entry:** suitable for the smallest companies, run as a once-a-year exercise — roughly one to two days of work.
- **ESG software:** platforms such as Workiva, Sphera, Watershed, or IBM Envizi, typically licensed at USD 30,000–150,000 per year. A practical choice for mid-sized and large companies.
- **ERP integration:** modules such as SAP Sustainability Control Tower or the Oracle ESG module, aimed at the largest companies, typically at USD 100,000+ per year.

For most Turkish SMEs, the natural progression is: year one on spreadsheets, years two and three on ESG software, and ERP integration from year four onward.

## Verification and error management

A carbon account is not a matter of self-assessment: assurance comes from third-party verification against ISO 14064-3 or the GHG Protocol. Companies typically obtain "limited assurance" in the first year and move toward "reasonable assurance" in later years. Verification fees generally fall in the USD 15,000–50,000 range annually, depending on company size, and are provided by independent assurance firms such as TÜV, Bureau Veritas, SGS, KPMG, EY, and Deloitte.

First-year errors are the norm. Typical mistakes include double-counting renewable-energy certificates, omitting refrigerant leaks, and double-counting within the Scope 3 spend-based calculation. The verification firm is expected to flag these during assurance.

## Conclusion

Carbon accounting looks complicated at first glance, but a disciplined, step-by-step build typically yields a functional infrastructure within six to twelve months. For manufacturers already navigating CBAM, these steps are already mandatory; for any company with EU customers, the same demand is likely to arrive in the near future.
