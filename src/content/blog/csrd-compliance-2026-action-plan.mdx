The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) has been entering into force in phases since 2024, and 2026 marks a critical threshold that will directly affect Turkish companies selling into the EU market or partnering with EU-headquartered firms. This article lays out a six-stage action plan for 2026.

## Stage 1: Determining CSRD scope

The first task is to clarify whether the company falls directly within CSRD scope. Direct applicability arises when a company meets two of three thresholds: an EU-based subsidiary with 250+ employees, net turnover above EUR 50 million, or total assets above EUR 25 million. Even companies that are not directly in scope may face indirect compliance pressure through the supply-chain reporting requests of large EU customers (for example, Volkswagen, Siemens, IKEA). A third category consists of companies that are publicly listed and generate liquidity within the EU.

Most Turkish exporters fall into the second category — not directly in scope, but subject to data requests transmitted down the customer chain. In this situation, a "Scope 3 support package" for the customer should be prepared by the end of 2026.

## Stage 2: Materiality assessment

CSRD is built on the concept of "double materiality": a company must separately report both how it is affected by sustainability matters (financial materiality) and how its own operations affect the environment and society (impact materiality). This requires a two-step analysis:

1. **Topic universe:** more than 70 topics across the 12 ESRS (European Sustainability Reporting Standards) — climate, pollution, water, biodiversity, the circular economy, workforce standards, consumer safety, business conduct, and more — are screened.
2. **Stakeholder consultation:** structured interviews or surveys are conducted with customers, employees, suppliers, investors, and civil-society representatives.

This process typically takes three to four months. It results in a list of roughly 15–25 "material" items, around which the substance of the report is organized.

## Stage 3: Data inventory and gap analysis

For each material topic, the ESRS specify the applicable reporting requirements in detail. The company must test whether it can meet those requirements with data it already holds. Three typical data categories emerge:

**Environmental:** Scope 1 (direct emissions), Scope 2 (purchased energy), Scope 3 (value-chain emissions), water use, waste generation, and biodiversity impact. Most Turkish companies can compile Scope 1–2 data, but Scope 3 is a substantial standalone data-collection undertaking.

**Social:** employee demographics, pay-equity metrics, health and safety statistics, training hours, and the human-rights conditions of suppliers.

**Governance:** board diversity, the proportion of independent directors, the linkage of executive remuneration to sustainability KPIs, ethics policies, and lobbying expenditure.

## Stage 4: Targets and action plans

CSRD does not merely require reporting historical data — it also calls for forward-looking targets. For each material topic, the company should define:

- A short-term target (1–2 years)
- A medium-term target (5 years, aligned to a 2030 horizon)
- A long-term target (net zero by 2050)
- Accountability for the target (which executive, which KPI)
- An annual action plan with a corresponding budget allocation

Targets must be realistic and calibrated to what the company can actually influence. Overreaching targets set without a credible pathway expose the company to greenwashing allegations and legal risk.

## Stage 5: Reporting infrastructure

The CSRD report must be filed electronically, in XBRL format, to the EU's Single Access Point. The company's infrastructure needs to be ready for this standardized format. There are two broad options: enterprise ESG software (Workiva, IBM Envizi, Sphera, and similar platforms) or an accounting-integrated spreadsheet model supported by external advisory services. The second approach tends to be more practical for small and mid-sized companies, while the first is effectively unavoidable at larger scale.

Automating the data-collection cycle reduces the risk of manual error and delay. ERP integration (SAP, Oracle) is critical for producing a reliable Scope 1–2 emissions calculation.

## Stage 6: Independent assurance

A CSRD report requires independent assurance — "limited assurance" initially, moving toward "reasonable assurance" over time. For Turkish companies, this typically means a new engagement with the ESG practice of their existing statutory auditor (a Big Four firm or a local equivalent). Assurance fees should be budgeted at roughly 40–60% of the existing financial-audit fee.

Typical first-year assurance findings include: insufficient Scope 3 data quality, sustainability policies that exist on paper but are not consistently implemented, targets that are not measurable, and stakeholder consultation that was superficial. These findings are generally remediated in the second year.

## Practical recommendations for Turkish companies

Three recommendations stand out:

1. **Start early.** Rather than reacting under pressure in the final quarter of 2026, build the reporting infrastructure incrementally throughout 2025. The first report is not expected to be perfect, but the underlying data infrastructure must already be in place.
2. **Prioritize customer requests.** If a major EU customer is already requesting Scope 3 data for its own report, that work materially accelerates — by a substantial margin — your own CSRD readiness.
3. **Bring in professional support.** Pairing the in-house team with ESG advisory support in the first year helps avoid the larger revision costs that tend to surface later.

## Conclusion

CSRD is a complex but manageable compliance project. A disciplined, six-stage approach leaves adequate time to prepare the end-2026 report. Companies that have not yet begun will find themselves under mounting pressure in 2027, heading toward a 2028 regulatory position from which there is effectively no way back.
