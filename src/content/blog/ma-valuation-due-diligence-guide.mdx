# M&A Due Diligence: Valuation Methodology for Corporate Mergers and Acquisitions

**Category:** M&A & Valuation | **Reading Time:** 9 min | **Author:** eCyPro Consulting

---

McKinsey & Company's comprehensive research finds that nearly 70% of M&A transactions fail to achieve the revenue synergies projected by the acquirer's management (Christofferson et al., 2004). Behind this striking statistic lie several recurring causes: inadequate due diligence, unrealistic valuation assumptions, and gaps in integration planning.

This guide examines the anatomy of successful M&A processes and offers corporate acquirers a comprehensive methodology.

---

## 1. M&A Strategy: Why Do We Acquire?

### 1.1 The Strategic Motivation Matrix

The decision to acquire should rest on one or more of the following strategic motivations:

| Motivation | Definition | Risk Level |
|------------|-------|-------------|
| **Market Entry** | Rapid access to a new geographic market | Moderate |
| **Talent Acquisition** | Critical human capital and know-how | High |
| **Product/Technology** | IP, patents, technology platform | High |
| **Consolidation** | Market share and cost synergies | Low–Moderate |
| **Vertical Integration** | Control over the supply chain | Moderate |

> **A critical caution:** The notion of "strategic fit" imperils the decision process whenever it is not translated into a concrete, measurable value-creation hypothesis (Porter, 1987).

### 1.2 Target Selection Criteria

Successful M&A processes begin with a systematic target-screening phase. eCyPro's **SCALAR** framework encompasses the following criteria:

- **S**ynergy Potential (revenue and cost)
- **C**ultural Fit
- **A**sset Quality
- **L**egal & Regulatory standing
- **A**ffordability (price reasonableness)
- **R**isk Profile

## 2. Valuation Methodologies

### 2.1 Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

DCF estimates a target company's intrinsic value by discounting its projected future cash flows to present value. The core formula:

```
DCF Value = Σ(FCFt / (1 + WACC)^t) + TV / (1 + WACC)^n
```

Where:
- **FCFt** = free cash flow in period t
- **WACC** = weighted average cost of capital
- **TV** = terminal value (Gordon growth model or exit multiple)

**Critical assumptions:** The WACC, the growth rate, and the terminal value are the most sensitive inputs in a DCF valuation. Scenario analysis (base, optimistic, pessimistic) is indispensable (Damodaran, 2012).

### 2.2 Comparable Company Analysis ("Comps")

Valuation derived from the trading multiples of similar publicly listed companies:

- **EV/EBITDA** (the most common)
- **EV/EBIT**
- **Price/Earnings (P/E)**
- **EV/Revenue** (for early-stage companies)

### 2.3 Precedent Transaction Analysis

An analysis of comparable, completed M&A transactions within the sector, incorporating the control premiums paid. From the acquirer's perspective, precedent-transaction multiples typically run 20–30% above comparable-company trading multiples (Rosenbaum & Pearl, 2013).

### 2.4 Synergy Valuation

Synergy value must be quantified separately, with explicit clarity on **when it will materialize, who is accountable for it, and what costs it will require.**

## 3. Due Diligence: A Comprehensive Checklist

### 3.1 Financial Due Diligence

**Core areas of review:**

- Normalized EBITDA calculation (stripping out one-off items)
- Working-capital analysis and seasonality
- Debt structure and contingent liabilities
- Earnings quality (customer concentration, contract structure)
- Cash-conversion cycle
- Tax position and tax exposures
- Related-party transactions

### 3.2 Legal Due Diligence

- Corporate structure and shareholder agreements
- Contracts (material supplier, customer, and lease agreements)
- Intellectual-property rights (patents, trademarks, copyrights)
- Pending litigation and contingent liabilities
- Regulatory compliance — including KVKK (Türkiye's Law on the Protection of Personal Data, No. 6698), competition law, and sector-specific licensing
- Employment agreements and collective-bargaining arrangements

### 3.3 Operational Due Diligence

- Sustainability of the business model
- Key-person dependency risk
- Technology infrastructure and cybersecurity
- Supply-chain quality and concentration risk
- Customer-churn rates
- Operational KPIs and benchmarking

### 3.4 Human-Resources Due Diligence

Cultural-fit assessment is the single most decisive factor in determining the success of the integration process (Bruner, 2004). HR due diligence should encompass:

- Retention risk within the management team
- Cultural-DNA analysis (values, decision-making style, leadership)
- Compensation and benefits benchmarking
- Organizational structure and role overlaps
- Turnover and employee-satisfaction indicators

## 4. Valuation Negotiation and the LOI

### 4.1 The Letter of Intent (LOI)

The LOI is a non-binding preliminary agreement. Even so, its core terms — the price range, deal structure, exclusivity period, and any conditions precedent — substantially shape the negotiation that follows.

**Key issues to resolve in the LOI:**
- Transaction consideration and payment structure (cash/stock/earn-out)
- Working-capital mechanism
- Seller representations and warranties
- Non-compete provisions
- Exclusivity period

### 4.2 Earn-Out Mechanisms

Earn-out structures are used to bridge valuation gaps between buyer and seller: additional consideration made contingent on the target's future performance. Earn-out disputes, however, are among the most common sources of M&A litigation; the measurement metrics, calculation method, and accounting policies must therefore be specified with exceptional precision (Datar et al., 2001).

## 5. Integration Planning (PMI)

### 5.1 Integration Approaches

| Model | Definition | Suited To |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Full Absorption** | Complete integration | Consolidation deals |
| **Best of Both** | Integration that selects the strongest elements of each organization | Deals of comparable scale |
| **Preservation** | Target kept independent | Technology/start-up acquisitions |
| **Transformation** | Both companies are reshaped | Platform-building deals |

### 5.2 The First 100 Days

The first 100 days constitute the critical phase of integration. During this period, acquirers should:

1. Identify and deliver quick wins
2. Retain key personnel
3. Reassure customers
4. Build cultural bridges between the two organizations
5. Track synergy realization
