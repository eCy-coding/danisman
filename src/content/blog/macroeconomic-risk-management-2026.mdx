Between 2024 and 2026, the global economic environment has presented corporate decision-makers with an unusual degree of uncertainty: the structural persistence of inflation in the United States, decelerating growth across Europe, continuing geopolitical crises in the Middle East and Ukraine, and structural transformation within the Chinese economy are together reshaping global trade flows in fundamental ways.

Firms operating in Türkiye must contend with these external shocks in addition to a distinct set of domestic macroeconomic risks arising from a high-inflation environment, exchange-rate volatility, and the ongoing normalization of monetary policy.

This analysis offers corporate CFOs and strategy leaders an actionable framework for macroeconomic risk management.

---

## 1. Mapping Macroeconomic Risk

### 1.1 Currency Risk

The Turkish lira lost substantial value against the US dollar over the 2021–2025 period. This depreciation sharpened balance-sheet and cash-flow risk for firms that borrow in foreign currency, rely on foreign-currency-denominated inputs, or export under foreign-currency contracts.

**Types of currency risk:**

| Risk Type | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Transaction Risk** | Exchange-rate movement on committed transactions | Payment of an import invoice |
| **Translation Risk** | Conversion of foreign-denominated assets into the reporting currency | Consolidated balance sheet |
| **Economic Risk** | Longer-run effect on competitive position | Market pricing power |

### 1.2 Inflation Risk

In a high-inflation environment, the principal mechanisms of corporate value erosion include:

- **Rising input costs:** Increases in raw material, energy, and labor costs compress margins.
- **Erosion of receivables:** Long-term, fixed-price contracts lose real value over their term.
- **Inventory valuation risk:** Under FIFO accounting, the sale of lower-cost inventory generates an accounting profit, even as replacement cost rises.
- **Difficulty in investment planning:** Cost-of-capital estimates and project-IRR assessments become considerably less certain.

### 1.3 Interest-Rate Risk

The Central Bank of the Republic of Türkiye's return to an orthodox monetary policy stance after 2023, followed by a gradual normalization of the policy rate, has created material interest-rate risk — particularly for firms carrying floating-rate debt (Central Bank of the Republic of Türkiye, 2024).

> **Key effects:** Rate increases raise the cost of borrowing, weigh on equity valuations, and reduce the market value of fixed-rate debt instruments.

## 2. Macroeconomic Scenario Planning

### 2.1 Scenario Methodology

The scenario-planning methodology pioneered at Shell remains one of the most tested frameworks for supporting strategic decision-making under uncertainty (Schwartz, 1991). Three baseline scenarios should be constructed:

**Base Scenario:** Built on central forecasts, assuming continuation of prevailing trends.

**Adverse Scenario:** A combination of a currency shock (depreciation in excess of 30%), renewed inflationary acceleration, and credit tightening.

**Favorable Scenario:** Inflation normalization, currency stability, and a recovery in investment appetite.

### 2.2 Stress-Test Parameters

The macroeconomic stress test eCyPro applies to corporate clients incorporates the following parameters:

- A USD/TRY shock of ±20%
- An inflation deviation of +5 percentage points
- An interest-rate shock of +300 basis points
- A GDP growth deviation of −2 percentage points
- An oil-price shock of ±30%

## 3. Financial Hedging Strategies

### 3.1 Currency Hedging Instruments

**Derivative Instruments:**

| Instrument | Application | Cost |
|------------|-------------|------|
| Forward Contract | Known currency exposure | Low |
| FX Option | Asymmetric protection | Moderate–High |
| Cross-Currency Swap | Long-term balance-sheet protection | Variable |
| Currency Swap | Short-term liquidity management | Low |

**Natural Hedging:** Matching revenue and expense currencies is the lowest-cost form of protection available to a firm. Where Turkish corporations match US-dollar revenue against US-dollar expenditure, the balance-sheet impact of exchange-rate volatility is reduced without recourse to derivative instruments.

### 3.2 Inflation-Hedging Strategies

- **Pricing power:** Price increases are more readily absorbed where brand strength is high and demand elasticity is low.
- **Shorter contract tenors:** Avoiding long-term, fixed-price agreements, or indexing them to inflation.
- **Real estate and real assets:** Investment in real assets provides a natural hedge against inflation.
- **Inventory policy:** Strategic management of inventory levels in an inflationary environment (buffer stock in place of strict just-in-time practice).

## 4. A Framework for Corporate Resilience

Building corporate resilience against macroeconomic shocks spans five dimensions:

### 4.1 Financial Resilience

- **Liquidity buffer:** Cash or short-term liquid assets covering, at minimum, three to six months of operating expenditure.
- **Debt-structure optimization:** Reducing floating-rate exposure, extending maturities, and planning refinancing in advance.
- **Pre-committed credit lines:** Ready access to committed credit facilities that can be drawn on during periods of stress.

### 4.2 Operational Resilience

- Supply-chain diversification (reducing dependence on any single supplier)
- Flexible production capacity (converting fixed costs into variable costs)
- Digital operating capability

### 4.3 Strategic Resilience

- A dynamic strategy-review cycle (moving from an annual plan to quarterly updates)
- Pivot capacity: organizational agility to adapt quickly to changing market conditions
- Optionality: the ability to condition growth or contraction decisions on how circumstances evolve

## 5. A Macroeconomic Monitoring Dashboard

Macroeconomic indicators that corporate CFOs should track on a regular basis:

**Türkiye-specific:**
- CBRT policy rate and inflation expectations
- USD/TRY and EUR/TRY spot and forward rates
- CDS spreads (credit default swap premia)
- Current account balance
- Rate of credit growth

**Global:**
- Federal Reserve and ECB interest-rate decisions
- The US 10-year Treasury yield
- Commodity indices (WTI/Brent, copper, wheat)
- The VIX (volatility index)
- PMI indicators (ISM, Eurozone)

---

## Conclusion

Macroeconomic risk is not something to be avoided; it is a strategic variable to be managed. Corporate resilience is the capacity to sustain performance in the face of shocks. Building that capacity requires treating early-warning systems, systematic scenario planning, effective hedging, and operational flexibility as parts of a single, integrated discipline.
